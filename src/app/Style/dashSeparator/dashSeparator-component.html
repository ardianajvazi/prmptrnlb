Dash Separator

em dash (—)

Also known as the long dash. It gets its name from its width—roughly the same as a capital letter m.

Em dashes are used with no space between the dash and the words surrounding them.

Use HTML code &mdash; or &#8212; to display an em dash.

Examples: Vantage Plus lets you choose benefits based on what's most important to you—cost, maximum choice of doctors, or a specific physician. Do yourself a favor—and reduce paper—by choosing to receive your Explanation of Benefits (EOB) electronically.

en dash (–)

Also known as the short dash. It gets its name from its width—roughly the same as an N.

The main use of the en dash is to connect ranges of numbers, dates, or days of the week, such as 2–50 or 1–100.

Avoid: Monday–Friday
(use "weekdays" instead)

Avoid: Monday–Wednesday
(use "Monday through Wednesday" instead)

Similar to ampersands, the use of the dash causes a cognitive load in the reader. Use "to" instead of an en dash. Exception: when real estate is very tight, such as tables or the mobile app.

If "from" precedes the range, use "to" between the numbers, instead of an en dash. Do not add a space on either side of a dash.

Incorrect: The plan covers employee groups with 2-50 members.

Avoid: The plan covers employee groups with 2–50 members.

Correct: The plan covers employee groups from 2 to 50 members.

En dashes should also be used when using compound modifiers.

Examples: Twenty Premera associates participated in a Biggest Loser competition from April 1 to May 15. Cell phone reception is unreliable along the North Korea–South Korea border. Policy details apply only to Premera Blue Cross–contracted providers.
